COPE: Chronic Observation and Progression Events Ontology

Abstract

Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) are increasingly being leveraged in healthcare to support the early detection, prediction, and personalized management of chronic diseases. Yet, their effective application remains limited by the absence of structured frameworks that unify diverse patient data, evolving disease trajectories, and computational methodologies. In this work, we present a comprehensive ontology designed to support health informaticians and data scientists in navigating this complex landscape. The ontology formalizes critical components of chronic disease modeling, including biological, behavioral, demographic, and geographical patient characteristics, modifiable and non-modifiable risk factors, symptom evolution, disease outcomes, and cycles of deconditioning. In parallel, it incorporates representations of AI/ML concepts such as modelling techniques, data modalities, sources, and problem formulation strategies. Crucially, the ontology embeds a temporal framework, allowing for the representation of timestamped observations and interventions supporting trajectory modelling from real-time and longitudinal data. It also models the provenance and context of AI/ML models, linking them to datasets, research publications, and domain-specific concepts. By providing a semantic foundation that bridges clinical knowledge and AI capabilities, this ontology enables the identification of data and methodological gaps, supports interpretability, and fosters the development of patient focused, data driven solutions. We demonstrate its utility through exemplar queries and use cases, offering it as a reusable resource for advancing the integration of AI in chronic disease care.

Introduction

Chronic diseases such as diabetes, cardiovascular conditions, and respiratory illnesses represent a significant burden on global healthcare systems, accounting for nearly 74% of all deaths worldwide [1]. Early detection and effective intervention are critical to managing these conditions and improving patient outcomes. In recent years, Artificial Intelligence (AI) and Machine Learning (ML) have emerged as powerful tools in this space, enabling prediction, stratification, and personalized care planning [2, 3]. However, despite the availability of increasingly rich datasets from sources such as Electronic Health Records (EHRs), wearable devices, and clinical imaging, the practical integration of AI/ML into chronic disease management remains a challenge.

A key barrier lies in the lack of semantic frameworks that formally connect patient characteristics, disease trajectories, risk factors, and computational methods. Health informaticians often struggle to navigate a fragmented landscape of data types, algorithms, and domain knowledge when designing or interpreting AI driven health systems. Ontologies, a formal and structured representation of knowl- edge [4], offer a promising solution by capturing the complex interdependencies among data, models in a machine interpretable way [5]. While several biomedical ontologies exist, such as Systematized Nomenclature of Medicine: Clinical Terms (SNOMED CT) [6], International Classification of Diseases (ICD) [7], and Fast Healthcare Interoperability Resources (FHIR) [8], they often lack support for repre- senting technical AI/ML dimensions such as modelling approaches, explainability mechanisms, or the evolving nature of wearable derived data [6].

To address this gap, we propose an ontology that provides a unified representation of patient centered disease modelling and AI/ML integration. This ontology is designed to support health informaticians and data scientists in selecting appropriate modelling techniques, identifying relevant data sources, and understanding the implications of patient specific attributes on prediction outcomes. It captures key elements such as biological, behavioral, psychographic, demographic, and geographical characteristics of patients, disease risk factors (including their modifiability), symptom profiles, and outcomes. Impor- tantly, it also includes classes and properties for AI/ML techniques (such as classification, regression, clustering), data nature (such as structured, time series), data sources (such as ECG, EHRs), and research outputs (such as datasets, papers, venues).

Crucially, the ontology introduces a temporal framework by modelling timestamped clinical observa- tions. Through the ObservationEvent class, it records the temporal context of symptoms, interventions, and risk exposure, enabling the representation of dynamic disease trajectories. This supports complex temporal queries such as: What symptoms manifested following a particular intervention? or How did a patientâ€™s risk profile evolve across disease stages?

The ontology also supports traceable and interpretable AI by embedding the provenance of compu- tational models including their training datasets, algorithmic strategies, and contextual usage within the same semantic space as clinical knowledge. AI models are connected to the specific data they consume, the techniques they employ, and the literature that supports their use, thereby aligning with the principles of transparency, reproducibility, and explainability in healthcare AI [9].

The proposed ontology aims to serve three purposes: (1) to support trajectory modelling of chronic diseases using continuous or episodic data from wearable devices, (2) to enable health informaticians and data scientists to systematically explore how data science can contribute to chronic disease prediction and management, and (3) to guide the development and evaluation of AI/ML methods that are interpretable, patient focused, and clinically actionable. We demonstrate the applicability of the ontology through a set of competency questions, use case examples, and exemplar queries.

Competency Questions

  1. Which patient characteristics are most strongly associated with increased susceptibility to specific diseases?
  2. Which population groups are most susceptible Diabetes Mellitus based on their characteristics?
  3. Given patient characteristics of age, what disease(s) are they at risk of developing in the near future?
  4. What are the risk factors associated with lung cancer?
  5. What diseases are patients at risk of, and what are the associated disease outcomes and identifying symptoms?
  6. Which AI/ML techniques are most commonly used for modeling disease progression?
  7. What machine learning models and input features are commonly used for predicting diseases such as Diabetes Mellitus?
  8. Which research articles focus on the detection or prediction of chronic diseases using AI/ML methods?
  9. For a given data source (ECG, EHR, wearable sensor data), which AI/ML techniques are most frequently applied?
  10. Which AI/ML modeling approaches are most commonly used to model disease progression in Hypertension?
